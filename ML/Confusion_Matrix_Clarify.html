🔍 Let's break them down:

✅ Accuracy

  • The ratio of correct predictions to total predictions.

  • Good when classes are balanced.

  • Problem: One class can dominate and inflate the score (like in your case).

📌 Precision

Out of all the predicted positives, how many were correct?

Formula:

Precision=True PositivesTrue Positives+False Positives\text{Precision} = \frac{\text{True Positives}}{\text{True Positives} + \text{False Positives}}Precision=True Positives+False PositivesTrue Positives​

✅ High precision = few false positives.

📌 Recall (a.k.a. Sensitivity or True Positive Rate)

Out of all actual positives, how many did we correctly predict?

Formula:

Recall=True PositivesTrue Positives+False Negatives\text{Recall} = \frac{\text{True Positives}}{\text{True Positives} + \text{False Negatives}}Recall=True Positives+False NegativesTrue Positives​

✅ High recall = few false negatives.

📌 F1-score

Harmonic mean of precision and recall. A balanced measure.

Formula:

F1-score=2×Precision×RecallPrecision+Recall\text{F1-score} = 2 \times \frac{\text{Precision} \times \text{Recall}}{\text{Precision} + \text{Recall}}F1-score=2×Precision+RecallPrecision×Recall​

✅ Best when you need a balance between precision and recall (especially with imbalanced classes).

Absolutely! Let’s explain precision and recall like you're hearing about them for the first time — using a real-world example and simple logic. 😊

🎯 Imagine this scenario:

You built a model to predict if an email is spam or not.

There are two possible outcomes for each email:

  • Spam (positive case)

  • Not Spam (negative case)

✅ Now, what do these terms mean?

🔹 True Positive (TP)

Email is spam, and your model says “spam” → ✅ Correct

🔹 False Positive (FP)

Email is not spam, but your model says “spam” → ❌ Wrong
(You wrongly marked a good email as spam)

🔹 False Negative (FN)

Email is spam, but your model says “not spam” → ❌ Wrong
(You missed a spam email)

🧠 Now let’s break down:

💡 Precision = How many of the predicted spams were actually spam?

It's about how accurate your positive predictions are.

🔸 In simple words:

Of all the emails your model marked as spam, how many were really spam?

So if your model said 10 emails were spam:

  • 9 were actually spam (✅ TP)

  • 1 was a good email (❌ FP)

Precision=99+1=0.9=90%\text{Precision} = \frac{9}{9+1} = 0.9 = 90\%Precision=9+19​=0.9=90%

Higher precision = fewer false alarms.

💡 Recall = How many of the actual spams were found by the model?

It’s about how well you catch all the real spam.

🔸 In simple words:

Of all the spam emails out there, how many did your model catch?

If there are 10 real spam emails:

  • Your model found 8 of them (✅ TP)

  • It missed 2 (❌ FN)

Recall=88+2=0.8=80%\text{Recall} = \frac{8}{8+2} = 0.8 = 80\%Recall=8+28​=0.8=80%

Higher recall = fewer misses.

🔁 Summary with emojis:

MetricFocus OnExampleIdeal For
🎯 PrecisionCorrectness of your positives"How many predicted spams were right?"When false positives are bad (e.g., don’t want to block good emails)
🔍 RecallCoverage of real positives"Did we catch all the spam?"When missing positives is bad (e.g., don't want to miss spam)

💡 Bonus:

If you're trying to balance both, that's where F1-score comes in — a mix of both precision and recall.
